\begin{abstract}
Quantum gravity is expected to render spacetime stochastic at the Planck scale.
We show that the resulting random arrival-time jitter affects the
field-correlation and intensity-correlation functions of light from distant
sources differently, breaking the Gaussian-statistics identity (the Siegert
relation) that links them for all chaotic-thermal light.
For spatially extended sources, foam-induced jitter between rays from different
parts of the source produces an additional suppression of the zero-lag
photon-bunching (Hanbury Brown--Twiss) peak that depends on the source angular
size and probes the transverse coherence length of the foam.
Both signatures are achromatic and follow a distance scaling distinct from
plasma scintillation or atmospheric turbulence, making them complementary to
the existing constraints from imaging and from energy-dependent time-of-flight
measurements.
We develop the framework in the narrow-band, weak-jitter, point-source limit
and its extension to spatially resolved sources, recover the published
Strehl-ratio and fringe-visibility tests as limiting cases, and quantify the
predicted observables for the Ng--Perlman family of foam models.
For the holographic exponent ($\alpha = 2/3$), the framework sets upper limits
on the line-of-sight foam path-length variance and on the transverse coherence
length that approach but do not clearly exceed the systematic floors
anticipated for next-generation optical intensity interferometers.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A long-standing expectation from the union of quantum mechanics and general
relativity is that the smooth pseudo-Riemannian manifold of classical spacetime
breaks down at the Planck length
$\ell_{\rm P} = \sqrt{\hbar G/c^3} \simeq 1.616\times 10^{-35}$~m.
In Wheeler's original ``spacetime foam'' picture~\cite{WHEELER1957604}, quantum
fluctuations of the metric on this scale render the geometry stochastic;
similar conclusions are reached in the Euclidean path-integral
treatment~\cite{Hawking1978} and in modern holographic and entropic-gravity
arguments from very different starting points.
While a complete theory of quantum gravity remains elusive, the question of
whether residual imprints of Planck-scale geometric fluctuations can accumulate
over cosmological baselines and become observable is one of the few empirical
handles on physics in the Planck regime, and it has motivated a substantial
body of phenomenological work over the past two
decades~\cite{Carlip2023,NgPerlman2022}.

A widely studied parametrization, due to Ng and
collaborators~\cite{Ng2001,Ng2003,NgPerlman2022}, assigns the cumulative
line-of-sight path-length variance acquired by light traversing a distance $D$
the power-law form
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_\ell^2(D) \;=\; A_\alpha\, D^{2(1-\alpha)}\, \ell_{\rm P}^{2\alpha},
  \label{eq:NgPerlmanIntro}
\end{equation}
where $A_\alpha$ is a dimensionless coefficient of order unity and the exponent
$\alpha$ encodes the underlying foam dynamics.
Three values are conventionally distinguished.
The ``conservative'' case $\alpha = 1$ follows from Wheeler's original
dimensional argument and gives $\sigma_\ell \sim \ell_{\rm P}$ regardless of
$D$ -- observationally negligible.
The ``holographic'' case $\alpha = 2/3$ is motivated by the requirement that
the information content of a region of spacetime saturate the
Bekenstein--Hawking bound and scale with the area of its boundary in Planck
units~\cite{Ng2001}; it predicts $\sigma_\ell \sim (D\ell_{\rm P}^2)^{1/3}$,
of order $10^{-15}$~m over $1$~Gpc.
The ``random-walk'' case $\alpha = 1/2$ treats successive Planck cells as
independent path-length kicks and gives
$\sigma_\ell \sim (D\ell_{\rm P})^{1/2}$, roughly $10^{-5}$~m over the same
distance -- large enough to randomize the optical phase of light from sources
at $z \gtrsim 1$.
The three cases bracket the range investigated most thoroughly; for a recent
review situating them within the broader quantum-gravity phenomenology
program, see Carlip~\cite{Carlip2023}.

The foam line of inquiry sits within a broader program of quantum-gravity
phenomenology that has matured substantially in the multi-messenger
era~\cite{Addazi2022,AlvesBatista:2023wqm}.
Other channels covered in those reviews include searches for
Lorentz-invariance violation in high-energy astrophysical transients, vacuum
birefringence over cosmological baselines, and modifications of
gravitational-wave propagation; complementary laboratory programs target
Planck-scale metric noise via tabletop interferometers~\cite{Verlinde:2019xfb},
and a parallel theoretical line asks whether quantum-gravitational degrees of
freedom decohere otherwise classical photon states during
propagation~\cite{Lagouvardos:2020laf}.
The present work belongs to the propagation-based, coherence-channel branch
of this landscape: we ask what imprint the Ng--Perlman foam ansatz leaves on
the first- and second-order temporal correlations of light from
cosmologically distant sources.

Two complementary observational channels have been used to test these models.
The first exploits the fact that random Planck-scale path-length deviations
imprint a stochastic phase $\sigma_\phi = 2\pi\sigma_\ell/\lambda$ across the
aperture of any telescope, reducing the Strehl ratio by
$S \approx \exp(-\sigma_\phi^2)$ and degrading the diffraction-limited
point-spread function.
Detections of crisp, near-diffraction-limited images of distant active galactic
nuclei with the Hubble Space Telescope and ground-based instruments
\cite{LieuHillman2003,2003ApJ...587L...1R,Christiansen2011,Perlman2015},
together with stellar interferometry, exclude $\alpha \lesssim 2/3$ at optical
wavelengths for $D \sim 1$--$10$~Gpc, leaving the holographic and conservative
cases as the only quantitatively viable models.
These exclusions assume transverse foam correlations are negligible on aperture
scales; models with long-range transverse correlations can evade blurring
constraints while retaining large $\sigma_\ell(D)$~\cite{LeeZurekChen2024}.
The second channel uses energy-dependent time-of-flight measurements of
high-energy astrophysical transients --- gamma-ray bursts observed by
Fermi~\cite{Fermi2009}, TeV blazar flares from H.E.S.S.~\cite{HESS2008}, and
LHAASO observations of GRB\,221009A~\cite{LHAASO2024}, currently the leading
limits --- to constrain the Lorentz-invariance-violating (LIV) dispersion
$\delta t \propto (E/E_{\rm QG})^n$ that some quantum-gravity models predict.
A recent analysis of GRB\,221009A claims consistency with holographic foam
blurring~\cite{SteinbringNg2026}, though without formal statistical
significance. Together the two channels have winnowed the parameter space
but not eliminated it, and several scalings --- including the holographic
$\alpha = 2/3$ --- remain unconstrained.

Both channels share a structural limitation that motivates the present work.
The imaging tests are essentially binary: once a near-diffraction-limited
image is registered the constraint saturates, and no further information
about the residual phase variance is recovered. The time-of-flight tests,
conversely, target a deterministic, energy-ordered delay --- precisely what
is \emph{not} expected from a geometric-optics path-length fluctuation
$\delta t=\Delta\ell/c$, which is frequency-independent. The stochastic,
achromatic spacetime foam jitter considered here therefore leaves no
LIV signal, and a foam model whose phase variance lies below the imaging
threshold is invisible to both channels. We address the natural question
of whether observables exist that bridge this gap --- sensitive to a phase
variance too small to erase fringes, but achromatic and thus distinguishable
from plasma scintillation or instrumental dispersion.

We develop such a framework based on the first-order (field--field) and
second-order (intensity--intensity) coherence functions of light from
astrophysical sources, denoted $g^{(1)}(\tau)$ and $g^{(2)}(\tau)$ in standard
quantum-optics notation.
The first-order coherence is equivalently the fringe visibility measured in a
stellar interferometer at delay $\tau$; the second-order coherence is the
intensity correlation measured in a Hanbury Brown--Twiss
experiment~\cite{HBT1956} (intensity correlations between two photon-counting
detectors, originally developed for stellar angular-diameter measurements).
For all stationary chaotic-thermal sources the two are linked by the
Siegert relation~\cite{Siegert1943}, the Gaussian-statistics identity
$g^{(2)}(\tau) - 1 = |g^{(1)}(\tau)|^2$.
The framework recovers the published imaging and fringe-visibility tests as
limiting cases of a single first-order observable, and we identify two
additional foam signatures inaccessible to those tests: one accessible to
single-aperture temporal coherence measurements on point-like sources, and
one to intensity interferometry on spatially resolved sources such as that
recently demonstrated by VERITAS~\cite{Abeysekara2020,Acharyya2024}.
Their derivation, observability, and use as discriminants against conventional
propagation effects are the subject of this paper.

The physical picture is simple: in the narrow-band limit, spacetime foam jitter
shifts the arrival time of a photon by $\delta t$ without appreciably changing
its intensity envelope. The first-order coherence $g^{(1)}$ measures
field--field correlations and so picks up the carrier-phase modulation
$e^{i\omega_0\delta t}$, which is exponentially suppressed when the rms
phase $\sigma_\phi=\omega_0\langle\delta t^2\rangle^{1/2}$ exceeds unity.
The second-order coherence $g^{(2)}$ measures intensity--intensity
correlations and is blind to that carrier phase. The two observables
therefore decouple, and the algebraic relation between them --- the
Siegert relation --- is broken in a foam-specific way.

The second-order observable proposed here is already routinely computed in
radio astronomy under different names (spectral kurtosis, modulation index;
Section~\ref{sec:radio}), so the framework translates directly to existing
data products.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:model} develops the model: the spatiotemporal covariance of
the foam-induced path-length fluctuation field, the differential-jitter
distribution at a single ray, and its generalization to ray pairs.
Section~\ref{sec:g1} treats the first-order coherence and its connection to
the published Strehl-ratio and fringe-visibility constraints.
Section~\ref{sec:g2} treats the second-order coherence, including its
conditional and unconditional forms in the narrow-band limit.
Section~\ref{sec:siegert} examines the Siegert relation under the foam-modified
field statistics.
Section~\ref{sec:extended} extends the analysis to spatially resolved sources,
derives the suppression of the zero-lag HBT bunching peak, and gives an
inversion procedure for the transverse foam coherence length $r_\perp$.
Section~\ref{sec:discriminants} identifies the distance scaling and
achromaticity that distinguish foam from conventional propagation phase
screens, and discusses the self-calibrating character of the second-order
observables --- features shared by both the point-source and extended-source
signatures.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusions} concludes.

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}
% ==============================================================

\subsection{Arrival-Time Jitter as a Time Reparameterization}

We model the detected field as a stochastically time-warped version of the
intrinsic source field:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\rm obs}(t) = E_{\rm src}\!\left(t - \delta t(t)\right),
  \label{eq:Eobs}
\end{equation}
where $E_{\rm src}(t)$ is the complex amplitude of the light at the source ---
modelled as a stationary zero-mean complex Gaussian process, the standard
description of a single chaotic/thermal mode --- and $\delta t(t)$ is the
small, time-dependent shift in arrival time picked up by a photon as it
traverses fluctuating Planck-scale geometry, treated as a stationary stochastic
process statistically independent of $E_{\rm src}$.
The jitter arises from cumulative path-length fluctuations along the
line of sight of propagation distance $D$:
\begin{equation}
  \delta t(t) = \frac{1}{c}\,\Delta\ell_{\rm tot}(t).
  \label{eq:jitter}
\end{equation}
In the geometric-optics limit the path-length shift $\Delta\ell_{\rm tot}$
is a purely geometric quantity and hence frequency-independent (achromatic),
in contrast to plasma dispersion which scales as $\lambda^2$.
This achromaticity is a key discriminant between foam and conventional
propagation effects.

\subsection{Spatial and Temporal Fluctuations of Spacetime Foam}
\label{sec:Calpha_origin}

Because foam is a property of spacetime rather than of any preferred direction,
the same dimensional logic that governs longitudinal path-length fluctuations
applies equally to transverse baselines. By dimensional analogy with the
longitudinal scaling, the transverse coherence has a characteristic length
scale $r_\perp$. We do not attempt to fix its value from first principles ---
the longitudinal scaling counts cells traversed along a ray, whereas the
transverse case mixes both the in-line distance $D$ and the cross-ray
separation $b$ in a way that is qualitatively distinct from the single-ray
accumulation --- and instead treat $r_\perp$ as a free parameter
(Section~\ref{sec:approx}), constrained empirically by the angular-size
dependence of $\delta g^{(2)}$ (Section~\ref{sec:extended}). We denote the
normalized transverse coherence function by $\Phi(b/r_\perp)$, satisfying
$\Phi(0)=1$ and $\Phi(b/r_\perp)\to 0$ for $b\gg r_\perp$, and treat it as a
free function; its model-dependence is discussed below.

Turning to the temporal behavior, quantum foam models posit that each Planck-scale cell of spacetime along the line
of sight fluctuates stochastically.
Two photons traversing the same cell at coordinate times separated by $\tau$
experience path-length fluctuations that are correlated for $\tau \ll \tau_{\rm foam}$
but decorrelated for $\tau \gg \tau_{\rm foam}$, where $\tau_{\rm foam}$ is the
characteristic temporal fluctuation timescale at each cell.
We introduce $C_\alpha(\tau;D)$ as the normalized \emph{temporal} autocorrelation
of the per-cell path-length fluctuation, integrated over the $\sim D/\ell_{\rm P}$
cells along the line of sight (where $\ell_{\rm P} = \sqrt{\hbar G/c^3}
\simeq 1.616\times10^{-35}$~m is the Planck length).
The timescale $\tau_{\rm foam}$ is an additional free parameter of the foam model,
not fixed by the Ng--Perlman variance relation alone.
If $\tau_{\rm foam}$ is of order the Planck time,
$\ell_{\rm P}/c \approx 5\times10^{-44}$~s, then $C_\alpha(\tau) \approx 0$ for all
accessible lags: the foam is fully decorrelated on any observable timescale, the most
optimistic scenario for detection.
In the opposite \emph{quasi-static} limit $\tau_{\rm foam} \gg D/c$ (foam coherent
across the entire line of sight), $C_\alpha(\tau) \approx 1$ for all $\tau$ of
interest and every temporal observable vanishes.

The Ng--Perlman variance scaling Eq.~\eqref{eq:NgPerlmanIntro} does not pin the
functional forms of $C_\alpha(\tau;D)$ and $\Phi(b/r_\perp)$, and specific foam
models predict different shapes.
The pixellon model~\cite{LeeZurekChen2024} gives a transverse structure with
long-range angular components that does not factorize as $\Phi(b/r_\perp)$
(Legendre polynomial series in aperture-plane angle, with the long-range
character strongly suppressing image blurring relative to the uncorrelated
case); holographic interferometric proposals in the Verlinde--Zurek
class~\cite{Verlinde:2019xfb} tie the metric-fluctuation noise spectrum to
the geometry of the propagation region through the holographic principle,
implying model-specific temporal correlation structure.
We keep $C_\alpha$ and $\Phi$ as free functions; quantitative comparison with
specific model-predicted kernels would require evaluating the convolution
integrals of Sections~\ref{sec:g1}--\ref{sec:extended} with those shapes,
which we leave to future work.

The full spatiotemporal covariance between two rays separated by transverse
distance $b$ takes the factorized form
\begin{equation}
  \big\langle \Delta\ell_{\rm tot}(t;\boldsymbol{\theta})\,
              \Delta\ell_{\rm tot}(t+\tau;\boldsymbol{\theta}')\big\rangle
  = \sigma_\ell^2(D)\,C_\alpha(\tau;D)\,
    \Phi\!\left(\frac{b(\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')}{r_\perp}\right),
  \label{eq:pathcov}
\end{equation}
where $b(\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}') \simeq D|\boldsymbol{\theta}-\boldsymbol{\theta}'|$.
The single-ray temporal covariance appropriate for a
point source is the special case
$\boldsymbol{\theta}=\boldsymbol{\theta}'$ (so $b=0$):
\begin{equation}
  \big\langle \Delta\ell_{\rm tot}(t)\,
              \Delta\ell_{\rm tot}(t+\tau) \big\rangle
  = \sigma_\ell^2(D)\,C_\alpha(\tau;D).
  \label{eq:pathcov1ray}
\end{equation}
The product form is a structural assumption of the model, discussed alongside
the other approximations in Section~\ref{sec:approx}. The two principal
observables developed below --- the Siegert discrepancy $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$ and
the zero-lag bunching suppression $\delta g^{(2)}$ --- both descend from
Eq.~(\ref{eq:NgPerlmanIntro}), with Sections~\ref{sec:g1}--\ref{sec:siegert}
probing its longitudinal projection and Section~\ref{sec:extended} its
transverse projection.

\subsection{Differential Jitter and Its Distribution}
\label{sec:differential_jitter}

With the spatiotemporal covariance model in place, we define the key derived quantity
that enters the coherence integrals.
Define the \emph{cross-ray differential jitter} between a photon arriving along
direction $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ at time $t+\tau$ and a photon arriving along
$\boldsymbol{\theta}'$ at time $t$:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_{\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}'}(\tau)
  \equiv \delta t(t+\tau;\boldsymbol{\theta}) - \delta t(t;\boldsymbol{\theta}').
  \label{eq:DeltaExt}
\end{equation}
For fixed $D$, stationarity implies the statistics depend only on $\tau$ and the
transverse separation $b(\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')=D|\boldsymbol{\theta}-\boldsymbol{\theta}'|$.
Expanding the square and applying Eq.~\eqref{eq:pathcov}:
\begin{align}
  \sigma_\Delta^2(\tau;\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')
  &= \frac{1}{c^2}\big\langle
    [\Delta\ell_{\rm tot}(t+\tau;\boldsymbol{\theta})
     - \Delta\ell_{\rm tot}(t;\boldsymbol{\theta}')]^2
    \big\rangle \notag \\
  &= \frac{1}{c^2}\left[
    \sigma_\ell^2 + \sigma_\ell^2
    - 2\sigma_\ell^2\,C_\alpha(\tau)\,\Phi\!\left(\frac{b}{r_\perp}\right)
    \right] \notag \\[4pt]
  &= \frac{2\sigma_\ell^2(D)}{c^2}
    \left[1 - C_\alpha(\tau;D)\,\Phi\!\left(\frac{b(\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')}{r_\perp}\right)\right].
  \label{eq:diffVarExt}
\end{align}
For $b\gg r_\perp$, $\Phi\to 0$ and the variance becomes
$2\sigma_\ell^2/c^2$ \emph{at all lags, including $\tau=0$} — a new effect
absent in the point-source case.

By the central limit theorem, $\Delta_{\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}'}(\tau)$
is a sum of contributions from many foam cells and is therefore Gaussian with zero mean:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_{\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}'}(\tau)
  \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,\sigma_\Delta^2(\tau;\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')\right),
  \label{eq:kernel}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_\Delta^2$ is taken from Eq.~\eqref{eq:diffVarExt}.
Limits of validity of this Gaussian approximation are collected in
Section~\ref{sec:approx}.

\subsection{Approximations, Limits, and Free Parameters}
\label{sec:approx}

The model defined by Eqs.~\eqref{eq:NgPerlmanIntro}, \eqref{eq:pathcov}, and
\eqref{eq:kernel} carries several structural approximations and free
parameters. We collect them here so they are not interleaved with the
derivations that follow.

\emph{Free parameters.} The variance coefficient $A_\alpha$, the temporal
correlation $C_\alpha(\tau;D)$ and timescale $\tau_{\rm foam}$, the transverse
coherence length $r_\perp$, the transverse coherence function $\Phi$, and (for
extended sources) the effective transverse factor $\Phi_{\rm eff}$ introduced
in Section~\ref{sec:PhiEff} parameterize distinct physical effects ---
single-ray variance, temporal foam dynamics, transverse coherence, and source
angular structure --- and are not claimed to be simultaneously determined by
any single observation.

\emph{Dynamical limit.} All numerical estimates in
Sections~\ref{sec:g1}--\ref{sec:extended} are evaluated in the dynamical limit
$C_\alpha\to 0$, which maximizes the predicted signal at any given lag. The
computed signals are therefore conditional upper bounds, not predictions of a
fully specified foam model.

\emph{Factorization of the covariance.} The product form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pathcov}
--- separating the total variance from the normalized spatiotemporal shape ---
is a structural assumption, not a consequence of the Ng--Perlman scaling; in a
general stationary process the variance and the normalized correlation functions
can carry independent $D$-dependence. Factorization carries a falsifiable
consequence: in the weak-jitter limit $\sigma_\phi\ll 1$, both the point-source
Siegert discrepancy $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$ (Section~\ref{sec:siegert}) and the
extended-source bunching suppression $\delta g^{(2)}$ (Section~\ref{sec:zerolag})
inherit their entire $D$-dependence from the single variance factor
$\sigma_\phi^2(D)\propto D^{2(1-\alpha)}$.
Because $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$ enters linearly in $\sigma_\phi^2$ while
$\delta g^{(2)}$ enters linearly in $\sigma_0\propto\sigma_\phi$
(Section~\ref{sec:zerolag}), factorization predicts
$\mathcal{S}(\tau)\propto D^{2(1-\alpha)}$ and
$\delta g^{(2)}\propto D^{1-\alpha}$ in the fully resolved limit
$\Phi_{\rm eff}\to 0$, where the geometric prefactor $1-\Phi_{\rm eff}\to 1$
is itself $D$-independent.
For partially resolved sources $\Phi_{\rm eff}$ depends on $D$ only through
the dimensionless combination $D\theta_{\rm src}/r_\perp$
(Section~\ref{sec:PhiEff}), so this combination must be held fixed when
distance-scanning a sample.
Subject to that condition, sources at a common $\alpha$ distributed in $D$
should trace two power laws whose $D$-exponents stand in the fixed ratio
$2:1$; a measured ratio inconsistent with $2:1$ at fixed $\alpha$ would
falsify factorization without requiring a specific non-factorized model.

\emph{Gaussianity and CLT breakdown.} The Gaussian kernel Eq.~\eqref{eq:kernel}
relies on the cells contributing to $\Delta_{\boldsymbol{\theta},
\boldsymbol{\theta}'}$ being approximately independent. For the cross-ray case
the two rays share foam cells along the line of sight, so independence requires
$b \gg r_\perp$. In the intermediate regime $b\sim r_\perp$ (equivalently
$r_\perp\sim D\Delta\theta$) the Gaussian approximation and the $\Phi_{\rm eff}$
reduction of Section~\ref{sec:PhiEff} break down together and introduce errors
of the same order; they must be assessed jointly.

\section{First-Order Coherence}
\label{sec:g1}
% ==============================================================

In Sections~\ref{sec:g1}--\ref{sec:siegert} we restrict to a point source ($b=0$),
for which the cross-ray differential jitter of Eq.~\eqref{eq:DeltaExt}
reduces to the single-ray differential jitter
\begin{equation}
  \Delta(\tau) \equiv \delta t(t+\tau) - \delta t(t),
  \label{eq:DeltaDef}
\end{equation}
with variance obtained from Eq.~\eqref{eq:diffVarExt} at $\Phi(0)=1$:
\begin{align}
  \sigma_\Delta^2(\tau;D)
  &= \frac{2\sigma_\ell^2(D)}{c^2}\big[1 - C_\alpha(\tau;D)\big].
  \label{eq:diffVar}
\end{align}

\subsection{Jitter Convolution Formula for $g^{(1)}$}

The observed first-order coherence is
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) = \frac{\langle E^*_{\rm obs}(t)\,E_{\rm obs}(t+\tau)\rangle}
                                  {\langle|E_{\rm obs}(t)|^2\rangle}.
  \label{eq:g1def}
\end{equation}
Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:Eobs} and letting
$u = t - \delta t(t)$, $v = t+\tau - \delta t(t+\tau)$, so that
$v - u = \tau - \Delta(\tau)$, stationarity of $E_{\rm src}$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \langle E^*_{\rm src}(u)\,E_{\rm src}(v)\rangle
  = \langle|E_{\rm src}|^2\rangle\,g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau - \Delta(\tau)).
\end{equation}
Since $\delta t$ is independent of $E_{\rm src}$, averaging over the jitter
yields
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)
    = \big\langle g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau - \Delta(\tau))\big\rangle_{\delta t}
    = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathcal{N}(\Delta;0,\sigma_\Delta^2(\tau))\,
      g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau - \Delta)\,d\Delta.
  \label{eq:g1obs}
\end{equation}
The observed first-order coherence is the intrinsic coherence function
convolved with the Gaussian differential-jitter kernel.

\subsection{Closed Forms for Lorentzian and Voigt Sources}

As a representative case admitting a closed-form result, we take a Lorentzian
line profile, which corresponds to an exponential coherence function via the
Wiener--Khinchin theorem:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau) = e^{-|\tau|/\tau_c},
  \label{eq:g1src_lorentz}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_c$ is the coherence time.
Evaluating Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1obs} by completing the square and splitting on the
sign of $\tau-\Delta$ gives, for $\tau > 0$:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)
  = e^{\sigma_\Delta^2/2\tau_c^2}
    \cdot\frac{1}{2}\left[
      e^{-\tau/\tau_c}\,{\rm erfc}\!\left(\frac{\sigma_\Delta/\tau_c - \tau/\sigma_\Delta}{\sqrt{2}}\right)
      + e^{\tau/\tau_c}\,{\rm erfc}\!\left(\frac{\sigma_\Delta/\tau_c + \tau/\sigma_\Delta}{\sqrt{2}}\right)
    \right].
  \label{eq:g1obs_erfc}
\end{equation}
In the weak-jitter limit $\sigma_\Delta \ll \tau_c$, the Gaussian average of
$e^{-|\tau-\Delta|/\tau_c}$ over $\Delta\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_\Delta^2)$
is dominated by $|\Delta|\ll|\tau|$, so one expands around $\Delta=0$:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) \approx e^{-|\tau|/\tau_c}
  \left[1 + \frac{\sigma_\Delta^2(\tau)}{2\tau_c^2} + \mathcal{O}(\sigma_\Delta^4)\right].
  \label{eq:g1weak}
\end{equation}
Here $\sigma_\Delta \equiv \sigma_\Delta(\tau)$ is $\tau$-dependent through $C_\alpha(\tau)$;
the expansion is in $\sigma_\Delta(\tau)/\tau_c$ at fixed $\tau$, and its validity
requires $\sigma_\Delta(\tau) \ll \tau_c$ at each lag of interest.
The correction is positive because $e^{-|\tau|/\tau_c}$ is convex and
Jensen's inequality applies.
The normalization $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(0) = 1$ is preserved exactly.

For a coherence function $g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau) = e^{-|\tau|/\tau_L}\,e^{-\tau^2/2\tau_G^2}$
whose Wiener--Khinchin transform yields a Voigt line profile
--- the convolution of a Lorentzian of width $\propto 1/\tau_L$ with a Gaussian of width $\propto 1/\tau_G$ modelling
Doppler and turbulent broadening --- the jitter kernel combines with the source Gaussian
into an effective width and auxiliary parameter,
\begin{equation}
  \tilde\sigma_1^2 = \frac{\sigma_\Delta^2\,\tau_G^2}{\sigma_\Delta^2+\tau_G^2},
  \qquad
  \mu_1(\tau) = \frac{\tau_G^2\,\tau}{\sigma_\Delta^2+\tau_G^2},
  \label{eq:voigt_params}
\end{equation}
giving, for $\tau > 0$:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)
  = \frac{\tilde\sigma_1}{\sigma_\Delta}\,
    e^{-\tau^2/2(\sigma_\Delta^2+\tau_G^2)}\,
    e^{\tilde\sigma_1^2/2\tau_L^2}\,
    \frac{1}{2}\!\left[
      e^{-\mu_1/\tau_L}\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\left(\frac{\tilde\sigma_1/\tau_L - \mu_1/\tilde\sigma_1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)
      +e^{\mu_1/\tau_L}\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\left(\frac{\tilde\sigma_1/\tau_L + \mu_1/\tilde\sigma_1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)
    \right].
  \label{eq:g1obs_voigt}
\end{equation}
The normalization $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(0)=1$ is preserved: as $\sigma_\Delta\to 0$,
$\tilde\sigma_1/\sigma_\Delta\to 1$, the exponential prefactors approach unity, and
the erfc combination gives 1.
In the limit $\tau_G\to\infty$ (pure Lorentzian), $\tilde\sigma_1\to\sigma_\Delta$ and
$\mu_1\to\tau$, so Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1obs_voigt} reduces to Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1obs_erfc}.
In the limit $\tau_L\to\infty$ (pure Gaussian), the erfc terms combine to unity and
$g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) = (\tilde\sigma_1/\sigma_\Delta)\,e^{-\tau^2/2(\sigma_\Delta^2+\tau_G^2)}$:
the observed coherence is a Gaussian whose width is broadened in quadrature by the jitter.

\subsection{Narrow-Band Limit}
\label{sec:narrowband}

For a quasi-monochromatic source with center frequency $\omega_0$ and
angular-frequency bandwidth $\Delta\omega \ll \omega_0$, write
$E_{\rm src}(t) = A(t)\,e^{-i\omega_0 t}$
where $A(t)$ varies on the coherence timescale
$\tau_c = 1/\Delta\omega = \lambda^2/(2\pi c\,\Delta\lambda)$.
When $\sigma_\ell/(c\tau_c) \ll 1$, typical realizations of $\delta t$ leave the
envelope unaffected, $A(t-\delta t) \approx A(t)$, and the jitter acts purely as
a carrier phase modulation. Intuitively, the slowly varying envelope $A(t)$
changes on the much longer coherence timescale $\tau_c$ and so is essentially
unmoved by a shift $\delta t \ll \tau_c$, while the rapidly oscillating carrier
$e^{-i\omega_0 t}$ rotates by $\omega_0\delta t$ --- a phase that can be of
order unity even when $\delta t/\tau_c$ is negligibly small. The observed
field therefore takes the form
\begin{equation}
  E_{\rm obs}(t) \approx A(t)\,e^{-i\omega_0 t}\,e^{i\omega_0\delta t(t)}.
  \label{eq:Eobs_narrowband}
\end{equation}
Corrections to this envelope approximation are of order
$(\sigma_\ell/c\tau_c)^2$: $\approx 0.28$ for $\alpha=1/2$ at the fiducial
parameters of Table~\ref{tab:comparison}, placing the random-walk case at
the edge of validity (where the exact convolution
Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1obs_erfc} should be used instead), and ${\sim}10^{-11}$
for $\alpha=2/3$, where the narrow-band derivation below is exact to any
practical precision.
Since $A(t)$ and $\delta t(t)$ are independent, the first-order coherence
factorizes.
The jitter contribution is the characteristic function of $\Delta(\tau)$
evaluated at $\omega_0$; since $\Delta(\tau)$ is Gaussian
(Section~\ref{sec:differential_jitter}),
\begin{equation}
  \big\langle e^{i\omega_0\Delta(\tau)}\big\rangle_{\delta t}
  = e^{-\omega_0^2\sigma_\Delta^2(\tau)/2}.
\end{equation}
This holds when $\tau \gg \tau_{\rm foam}$ so that foam-cell contributions are
independent; in the dynamical limit $\tau_{\rm foam} \ll \tau_c$ the condition
is satisfied at all observationally relevant lags.
Substituting $\omega_0 = 2\pi c/\lambda$ and Eq.~\eqref{eq:diffVar}:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)
    = g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)\cdot
      \exp\!\left(-\sigma_\phi^2\big[1 - C_\alpha(\tau;D)\big]\right),
  \label{eq:g1narrowband}
\end{equation}
where we define the rms accumulated phase
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_\phi \equiv \frac{2\pi\,\sigma_\ell(D)}{\lambda}
  = \frac{2\pi\,\sqrt{A_\alpha}\,D^{1-\alpha}\,\ell_{\rm P}^\alpha}{\lambda}.
  \label{eq:sigmaphi}
\end{equation}
We write the foam suppression factor as
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(\tau) \equiv e^{-\sigma_\phi^2[1-C_\alpha(\tau;D)]},
  \label{eq:Ffoam}
\end{equation}
so $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) = g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)\cdot\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(\tau)$.
At $\tau=0$: $\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(0) = 1$.
At $\tau\to\infty$: $\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(\infty) = e^{-\sigma_\phi^2[1-C_\alpha(\infty;D)]}$,
which equals $e^{-\sigma_\phi^2}$ in the dynamical limit ($C_\alpha\to 0$)
and approaches unity for quasi-static foam ($C_\alpha\approx 1$).

\subsection{Regime Summary}
\label{sec:regime_summary}

The exact convolution Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1obs} (and its Lorentzian closed form
Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1obs_erfc}) is always valid within the model.
The weak-jitter expansion Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1weak} requires
$\sigma_\Delta(\tau)/\tau_c\ll 1$;
the narrow-band carrier-phase result Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1narrowband} requires
$\sigma_\ell/(c\tau_c)\ll 1$ and captures the dominant physics when
$\sigma_\phi\gtrsim 1$ but $\sigma_\phi\ll\lambda/\Delta\lambda$.
The two conditions are compatible and govern distinct effects (envelope
displacement vs.\ carrier-phase suppression); when neither holds,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1obs} must be evaluated directly.

% ==============================================================
\subsection{Connection to Fringe Visibility and Strehl Ratio}
\label{sec:connection}
% ==============================================================

The fringe visibility and Strehl ratio are the observables through which
published constraints on spacetime foam have been obtained~\cite{LieuHillman2003,NgPerlman2022,Perlman2015,Christiansen2011,2003ApJ...587L...1R}.
Both are direct consequences of the first-order coherence $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)$
derived above, and the foam suppression factor $\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(\tau)$
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:Ffoam} unifies them in a single framework.

\subsubsection{Fringe Visibility and the Lieu--Hillman Condition}

The fringe visibility measured in a stellar interferometer at time delay $\tau$
is $V(\tau) = |g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)|$.
From Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1narrowband}:
\begin{equation}
  V(\tau) = V_{\rm src}(\tau)\cdot\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(\tau),
  \label{eq:visibility}
\end{equation}
where $V_{\rm src}(\tau) = |g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)|$ is the intrinsic source
visibility.
Lieu \& Hillman~\cite{LieuHillman2003} argue that fringes are erased when the
accumulated phase randomizes over $[0,2\pi)$, i.e.\ $\sigma_\phi \gtrsim 1$ ---
the regime in which the rms accumulated phase exceeds one radian and
randomizes the phase across the aperture, erasing interferometric fringes.
In our language this is the limit $\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam} \to 0$:
the foam suppression factor collapses and $V \to 0$.
The Lieu--Hillman condition $\Delta\phi \geq 2\pi$, where
$\Delta\phi \simeq 2\pi\sigma_\ell(D)/\lambda$~\cite{NgPerlman2022}, is
precisely $\sigma_\phi \geq 1$.
Our result extends this binary criterion to a continuous suppression curve
$V(\tau)$ whose shape encodes the temporal decorrelation $C_\alpha(\tau;D)$.

\subsubsection{Strehl Ratio}

The Strehl ratio $S$ — the ratio of the observed PSF peak to the
diffraction-limited peak — is the standard measure of wavefront quality in
telescope optics.
In the Ng--Perlman picture~\cite{NgPerlman2022}, spacetime foam imprints a
spatially varying random phase $\Delta\phi(x,y)$ across the entrance aperture:
each point $(x,y)$ on the mirror receives a ray that has accumulated an
independent random path-length shift from the Planck-scale cells it traversed,
producing a random phase screen with rms value $\sigma_\phi$.
When these phase shifts are spatially uncorrelated across the aperture — the
limit $r_\perp \ll D_{\rm tel}$, where $r_\perp$ is the transverse foam
coherence length and $D_{\rm tel}$ is the aperture diameter — the
Mar\'{e}chal approximation (the small-phase-variance expansion of the
diffraction-limited PSF reduction) gives
\begin{equation}
  S = e^{-\sigma_\phi^2},
  \label{eq:Strehl}
\end{equation}
valid for $\sigma_\phi \lesssim 1$; for $\sigma_\phi \gg 1$ the PSF peak is
effectively destroyed.

The same $\sigma_\phi^2$ appears in the temporal framework as the large-lag
limit of $\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}$. In the dynamical limit
$C_\alpha(\infty;D)\to 0$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:Ffoam} gives
\begin{equation}
  S = \mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(\tau\to\infty) = e^{-\sigma_\phi^2},
  \label{eq:StrehlFoam}
\end{equation}
so the spatial and temporal descriptions reduce to the same Gaussian
characteristic function: summing independent phase contributions across the
aperture at a fixed time, or tracking the phase along a single ray as the lag
grows large enough that the two time samples become independent. For
quasi-static foam ($C_\alpha\approx 1$) the temporal result instead gives
$\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(\infty)\to 1$, consistent with $S\to 1$ in the
$r_\perp \gg D_{\rm tel}$ limit.

The two channels do, however, depend differently on $r_\perp$. The Maréchal
formula \eqref{eq:Strehl} requires adjacent aperture elements to sample
\emph{independent} foam realizations, i.e.\ $r_\perp \ll D_{\rm tel}$; in the
opposite limit $r_\perp \gg D_{\rm tel}$ the wavefront acquires a common
time-varying phase offset with no spatial variation and $S\to 1$ even when
$\sigma_\phi^2$ is large. The temporal $g^{(1)}$ of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1narrowband}, by contrast, probes the phase along a single ray
as a function of lag and is insensitive to $r_\perp$ relative to the aperture.
The transverse coherence function $\Phi(b/r_\perp)$ governs a physically
distinct effect --- correlations between rays from different sky directions
--- which manifests in the second-order coherence of extended sources
(Section~\ref{sec:extended}).


% ==============================================================
\section{Second-Order Coherence}
\label{sec:g2}
% ==============================================================

We derive the second-order coherence $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)$ for a point source,
continuing the framework of Section~\ref{sec:g1}.
The result has the same structure as Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1obs}: $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}-1$
is a convolution of $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}-1$ with the identical Gaussian jitter kernel.
Because the intensity $|E_{\rm obs}|^2$ is blind to the carrier phase
$e^{i\omega_0\delta t}$, foam leaves $g^{(2)}$ nearly unchanged in the narrow-band
limit while strongly suppressing $g^{(1)}$ --- the asymmetry that produces the
Siegert violation developed in Section~\ref{sec:siegert}.

\subsection{Jitter Convolution Formula for $g^{(2)}$}

Fix a realization of the jitter process $\delta t(\cdot)$.
Conditioned on it, $E_{\rm obs}(t)$ is a deterministic time-warp of a
complex Gaussian field, so the Siegert identity holds at each fixed pair
$(t,t+\tau)$ as a purely algebraic Gaussian moment identity, with no
stationarity required.
(Ergodicity of $E_{\rm src}$ allows the conditional time-average to be
replaced by the conditional ensemble average; the subsequent average over
the stationary $\delta t$ process then yields a function of $\tau$ only,
by independence of $E_{\rm src}$ and $\delta t$.)
With $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau\mid\delta t) = g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau-\Delta(\tau))$
and the intrinsic Siegert relation $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}-1 = |g^{(1)}_{\rm src}|^2$:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau\mid\delta t) - 1
  = g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\tau-\Delta(\tau)) - 1.
\end{equation}
Averaging over the jitter distribution:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) - 1
    = \big\langle g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\tau-\Delta(\tau))-1\big\rangle_{\delta t}
    = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathcal{N}(\Delta;0,\sigma_\Delta^2(\tau))\,
      \big[g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\tau-\Delta)-1\big]\,d\Delta.
  \label{eq:g2obs}
\end{equation}
This Gaussian kernel relies on the CLT argument of
Section~\ref{sec:differential_jitter}, which requires $\tau \gg \tau_{\rm foam}$;
for $\tau \lesssim \tau_{\rm foam}$ the kernel may deviate from Gaussian and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2obs} should be regarded as approximate in that regime
(see Section~\ref{sec:Calpha_origin} for the definition and limits of $\tau_{\rm foam}$).

\subsection{Narrow-Band Limit: $g^{(2)}$ is Unchanged to Leading Order}

The intensity $I_{\rm obs}(t)=|E_{\rm obs}(t)|^2$ is blind to the carrier phase
$e^{i\omega_0\delta t(t)}$: only the envelope matters.
In the narrow-band limit $\delta t \ll \tau_c$, the jitter does not appreciably
shift the envelope, $A(t-\delta t)\approx A(t)$, so
$I_{\rm obs}(t)\approx|A(t)|^2=I_{\rm src}(t)$ to leading order in $\delta t/\tau_c$.
Consequently:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) = g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\tau)
  \qquad \text{(leading order in $\delta t/\tau_c$)},
  \label{eq:g2unchanged}
\end{equation}
with corrections of order $(\sigma_\ell/c\tau_c)^2$ from both envelope-timing
and carrier-phase contributions to the intensity, of the same magnitude as
those discussed for $g^{(1)}$ in Section~\ref{sec:narrowband}: at the ${\sim}8\%$
level for the (already excluded) $\alpha=1/2$ benchmark and ${\sim}10^{-11}$ for
$\alpha=2/3$.

By contrast, $g^{(1)}$ \emph{is} sensitive to the carrier phase because
$\sigma_\phi = \omega_0\sigma_\ell/c$ can be large even when $\delta t \ll \tau_c$:
at the same fiducial parameters, $\sigma_\phi \approx 280 \gg 1$.

\section{Siegert Relation Violation}
\label{sec:siegert}

The Siegert relation $g^{(2)}(\tau)-1=|g^{(1)}(\tau)|^2$ holds for any
stationary complex Gaussian field and provides a powerful internal consistency
check: once $g^{(1)}$ is known, the expected $g^{(2)}$ is fixed.
As foreshadowed in Section~\ref{sec:g2}, foam violates this relation because
$g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}$ is suppressed by the carrier-phase factor
$\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}$ while $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}$ is not.
This section derives and characterizes the Siegert discrepancy
$\mathcal{S}(\tau) \equiv [g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}-1] - |g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}|^2$.

\subsection{General Bound}

Define the Siegert discrepancy:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{S}(\tau)
  \equiv \left[g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)-1\right] - \left|g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)\right|^2.
  \label{eq:Sdef}
\end{equation}
Let $X \equiv g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau-\Delta(\tau))$, a complex random variable.
From Eqs.~\eqref{eq:g1obs} and \eqref{eq:g2obs}:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) = \langle X\rangle_{\delta t}, \qquad
  g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) - 1 = \langle |X|^2\rangle_{\delta t}.
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{S}(\tau)
  = \langle |X|^2\rangle - |\langle X\rangle|^2
  \geq 0,
  \label{eq:Svar}
\end{equation}
by the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality, with equality only when $\Delta(\tau)$ has
zero variance (i.e., at $\tau=0$ or in the absence of foam).
The Siegert relation is violated for all $\tau\neq 0$ whenever foam is present.
This non-negativity holds for a point source; for extended sources the
cross-ray differential jitter can drive $\mathcal{S}(0)<0$
(see Section~\ref{sec:extended}).
The Siegert violation is not a non-classical signature: the foam-modified field
remains a classical stochastic process, and the classical inequalities
$g^{(2)}(0)\geq g^{(2)}(\tau)$ and $g^{(2)}(0)>1$ are preserved; it is only
the Gaussian-statistics identity relating $g^{(2)}$ to $g^{(1)}$ that is broken.

\subsection{Sensitivity Scale Decoupling}
\label{sec:scales}

The violation arises because $g^{(1)}$ and $g^{(2)}$ respond to foam on
different scales.
The first-order coherence is controlled by $\sigma_\phi =
2\pi\sigma_\ell(D)/\lambda$, the rms accumulated phase along a single ray;
$g^{(1)}$ is sensitive to it because the carrier phase $e^{i\omega_0\delta t}$
appears directly in the field correlation.
The second-order coherence is controlled by $\sigma_\Delta(\tau)/\tau_c$,
the rms differential jitter scaled by the coherence time; $g^{(2)}$ is
sensitive to it because intensity correlations depend on how much the
jitter \emph{changes} between two detection events, not its absolute value.
These two scales are related by
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\sigma_\Delta(\tau)}{\tau_c}
  = \frac{\sqrt{2[1-C_\alpha(\tau)]}\,\sigma_\ell(D)}{c\tau_c}
  = \sigma_\phi \cdot \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda}\cdot\sqrt{2[1-C_\alpha(\tau)]},
  \label{eq:ratio}
\end{equation}
using $\omega_0\tau_c = \lambda/\Delta\lambda$.
The factor $\Delta\lambda/\lambda \ll 1$ means that even when $\sigma_\phi \gg 1$
(fringes erased), $\sigma_\Delta(\tau)/\tau_c$ can be small — so $g^{(2)}$ remains
near its intrinsic value while $g^{(1)}$ is suppressed.
This decoupling is the physical root of the Siegert violation, and is only
possible within the narrow-band regime discussed in Section~\ref{sec:narrowband}.

\subsection{Explicit Form and Properties in the Narrow-Band Limit}

In the narrow-band limit, $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs} \approx g^{(2)}_{\rm src}$
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2unchanged}), while $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}$ is suppressed by
$\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}(\tau)$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1narrowband}).
Using the intrinsic Siegert relation $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\tau)-1=|g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)|^2$,
to leading order:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{S}(\tau) \approx
  |g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)|^2\!\left(1-e^{-2\sigma_\phi^2[1-C_\alpha(\tau;D)]}\right).
  \label{eq:Sexplicit}
\end{equation}
In the weak-jitter limit $\sigma_\phi\ll 1$,
$\mathcal{S}\approx 2\sigma_\phi^2[1-C_\alpha]\,|g^{(1)}_{\rm src}|^2$ — linear
in $\sigma_\phi^2$ and small.
In the strong-jitter limit $\sigma_\phi\gg 1$, the exponential vanishes and
$\mathcal{S}\approx|g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)|^2$: $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}-1$ retains
its intrinsic value while $|g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}|^2\to 0$, producing a full violation.
Quasi-static foam ($C_\alpha\approx 1$) gives $\mathcal{S}\approx 0$ at all lags
— foam is invisible to all coherence observables in this limit.
The discrepancy $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$ is a direct observable — both
$g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}$ and $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}$ are measurable from the same
dataset — and the modulation beyond the prefactor $|g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)|^2$
is controlled solely by $\alpha$, $D$, and $C_\alpha(\tau;D)$, independently
of the unknown source spectrum.

% ==============================================================
\section{Extended Sources: Transverse Foam Coherence}
\label{sec:extended}
% ==============================================================

We now generalize from a point source to an extended incoherent source.
The first-order coherence $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}$ turns out to be unchanged from the
point-source result, because $g^{(1)}$ probes only single-ray statistics.
The second-order coherence $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}$, however, acquires new structure
through cross-ray differential jitter between rays arriving from different sky
directions, producing a suppression of the zero-lag bunching peak that directly
probes the transverse foam coherence length $r_\perp$.

\subsection{Field Model and Spatiotemporal Covariance}

We generalize to an extended incoherent source whose angular brightness
distribution is $I(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ (normalized to unity).
The analysis assumes a point (single spatial mode) detector; a finite aperture
would introduce Van~Cittert--Zernike geometric phases that modify the angular
coherence.
The detected field is a weighted superposition over rays labelled by direction
offset $\boldsymbol{\theta}$:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\rm obs}(t) = \int d\boldsymbol{\theta}\,I^{1/2}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\,
  E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\!\left(t - \delta t(t;\boldsymbol{\theta})\right),
  \label{eq:EobsExt}
\end{equation}
where the $E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ are independent, identically distributed
complex Gaussian processes (one per sky direction), and each ray carries its
own spacetime foam jitter $\delta t(t;\boldsymbol{\theta})$.
The spatiotemporal covariance of the path-length aggregate is given by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:pathcov} of Section~\ref{sec:model}, with
$b(\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}') \simeq D|\boldsymbol{\theta}-\boldsymbol{\theta}'|$
the transverse separation between rays evaluated at the source end.
For foam distributed uniformly along the line of sight the ray separation at
position $z$ is $z|\boldsymbol{\theta}-\boldsymbol{\theta}'|$, so the full
cross-covariance integral involves an average over $0\leq z\leq D$;
setting $b = D|\boldsymbol{\theta}-\boldsymbol{\theta}'|$ evaluates the separation
at the source plane.
Because $r_\perp$ is treated as an effective free parameter, the $O(1)$ geometric
factor from the $z$-integral is absorbed into its definition; its value is
constrained empirically by the angular-size dependence of $\delta g^{(2)}$,
derived in Section~\ref{sec:PhiEff}.

\subsection{First-Order Coherence is Unchanged}

The numerator of $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)$ involves cross terms
$\langle E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\ldots)\,E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}'}(\ldots)\rangle$.
Because different source directions are spatially incoherent,
\begin{equation}
  \langle E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(s)\,E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}'}(s')\rangle
  \propto \delta(\boldsymbol{\theta}-\boldsymbol{\theta}'),
\end{equation}
so only the diagonal $\boldsymbol{\theta}=\boldsymbol{\theta}'$ contributes.
Each diagonal term reproduces the single-ray result of
Section~\ref{sec:narrowband}, and the angular integral over $I$ gives unity.
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)
  = g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)\cdot e^{-\sigma_\phi^2[1-C_\alpha(\tau;D)]},
  \label{eq:g1ext}
\end{equation}
identical to the point-source result Eq.~\eqref{eq:g1narrowband}.
The first-order coherence probes only single-ray statistics; the transverse
foam structure leaves no imprint on $g^{(1)}$.

\subsection{Second-Order Coherence for an Extended Source}

The unnormalized intensity correlator is
$\langle I(t)I(t+\tau)\rangle = \langle E^*(t)E(t)E^*(t+\tau)E(t+\tau)\rangle$,
where $E \equiv E_{\rm obs}$ is the field of Eq.~\eqref{eq:EobsExt}.
Expanding the double angular integral and applying the Wick (moment) theorem
to the four-point function of the zero-mean complex Gaussian field
$E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ produces two pairings (the $I^{1/2}$ weight factors
enter the angular integral and are suppressed here for clarity):
\begin{align}
  &\langle E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_1}E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_2}
            E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_3}E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_4}\rangle
  =
  \langle E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_1}E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_2}\rangle
  \langle E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_3}E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_4}\rangle
  +
  \langle E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_1}E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_4}\rangle
  \langle E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_3}E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_2}\rangle.
  \label{eq:Wick}
\end{align}
The first pairing contributes $\langle I(t)\rangle\langle I(t+\tau)\rangle$
(the disconnected term, which cancels when forming $g^{(2)}-1$).
The second pairing gives the connected contribution.
Because different source directions are spatially incoherent,
$\langle E^*_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(s)\,E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}'}(s')\rangle
\propto \delta(\boldsymbol{\theta}-\boldsymbol{\theta}')$,
so the connected pairing requires $\boldsymbol{\theta}_1=\boldsymbol{\theta}_4$
and $\boldsymbol{\theta}_3=\boldsymbol{\theta}_2$.
The four-fold angular integral therefore collapses to a double integral
over ray pairs $(\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')$, each pair
contributing its own jitter-convolved $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}$:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) - 1
  = \int\!\!\int d\boldsymbol{\theta}\,d\boldsymbol{\theta}'\;
    I(\boldsymbol{\theta})I(\boldsymbol{\theta}')
    \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}
      \mathcal{N}\!\left(\Delta;0,\sigma_\Delta^2(\tau;\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')\right)\,
      \big[g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\tau-\Delta)-1\big]\,d\Delta,
  \label{eq:g2obsExt}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_\Delta^2(\tau;\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')$ is from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:diffVarExt} (Section~\ref{sec:differential_jitter}).
The key new feature relative to the point-source case is that for $b\gg r_\perp$,
$\Phi\to 0$ and $\sigma_\Delta^2\to 2\sigma_\ell^2/c^2$ at all lags, including
$\tau=0$.
In the limit $\Phi\equiv 1$ (all rays transversely coherent),
every kernel reduces to the single-ray $\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_\Delta^2(\tau))$ and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2obsExt} reduces to the point-source result Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2obs}.

\subsection{Effective Transverse Coherence Factor}
\label{sec:PhiEff}

To reduce Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2obsExt} to a tractable form, define the
effective transverse coherence factor:
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\rm eff}
  \equiv \int\!\!\int d\boldsymbol{\theta}\,d\boldsymbol{\theta}'\;
         I(\boldsymbol{\theta})I(\boldsymbol{\theta}')\,
         \Phi\!\left(\frac{b(\boldsymbol{\theta},\boldsymbol{\theta}')}{r_\perp}\right).
  \label{eq:PhiEff}
\end{equation}
This replaces the ray-dependent differential variance with a single effective
variance:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\tau,\rm eff}^2(\tau)
  \equiv \frac{2\sigma_\ell^2(D)}{c^2}
         \Big[1 - C_\alpha(\tau;D)\,\Phi_{\rm eff}\Big],
  \label{eq:sigmaEff}
\end{equation}
and allows Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2obsExt} to be approximated by the simpler
1D convolution of Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2obs} with $\sigma_\Delta^2$ replaced by
$\sigma_{\tau,\rm eff}^2$.
The two limits are:
$\Phi_{\rm eff}\to 1$ (point-like source or $r_\perp\gg D\,\Delta\theta$)
recovers the single-ray result; $\Phi_{\rm eff}\to 0$ (fully incoherent,
$r_\perp\ll D\,\Delta\theta$) maximizes the effective variance.
The approximation becomes exact in both limits; the relative error of the
single-kernel approximation is bounded above by
$(1-\Phi_{\rm eff})/\Phi_{\rm eff}$, peaking at $\sim 50\%$ near
$\Phi_{\rm eff}=1/2$ ($D\theta_{\rm src}\sim r_\perp$) where the cross-ray
Gaussian approximation also degrades (Section~\ref{sec:approx}). For the
resolved-limit applications of Section~\ref{sec:rperp_inversion},
$\Phi_{\rm eff}\ll 1$ and the single-kernel approximation is controlled.

\subsection{Extended-Source Siegert Discrepancy and Zero-Lag Bunching}
\label{sec:zerolag}

For the extended source, $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)$ is unchanged from the
point-source result (Eq.~\ref{eq:g1ext}), while $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)$
is given by the general mixture Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2obsExt}.
Unlike the point-source case, in the narrow-band limit $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}$
is \emph{not} equal to $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}$: the cross-ray differential jitter
modifies the intensity correlation through the envelope timing, even though
the carrier phase does not affect individual intensities.
The Siegert discrepancy is therefore
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{S}(\tau)
  = \left[g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau) - 1\right]
    - \left|g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)\right|^2,
  \label{eq:SextDef}
\end{equation}
where $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)-1$ is given by Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2obsExt} and
$|g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(\tau)|^2 = |g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)|^2\,e^{-2\sigma_\phi^2[1-C_\alpha(\tau)]}$.

At $\tau=0$, $g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(0)=1$ exactly by the spatial-incoherence argument of
Section~\ref{sec:extended}, so $\mathcal{S}(0) = g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(0) - 2$.
Evaluating the zero-lag effective variance with $C_\alpha(0;D)=1$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_0^2
  \equiv \frac{2\sigma_\ell^2(D)}{c^2}(1-\Phi_{\rm eff}),
  \label{eq:sigma0}
\end{equation}
which is strictly positive whenever $\Phi_{\rm eff}<1$, so the ray-pair mixture
reduces to a single Gaussian kernel of width $\sigma_0$.

For a Lorentzian source, $g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau) = e^{-|\tau|/\tau_c}$,
so the Siegert relation gives $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\tau) - 1 = e^{-2|\tau|/\tau_c}$.
The zero-lag convolution over $\Delta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_0^2)$
then becomes, setting $s \equiv \sigma_0/\tau_c$ and splitting on the sign
of $\Delta$ (both halves contributing equally by symmetry of $|\Delta|$),
\begin{equation}
  \big\langle e^{-2|\Delta|/\tau_c}\big\rangle
  = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_0}\int_0^\infty
    e^{-2\Delta/\tau_c}e^{-\Delta^2/2\sigma_0^2}d\Delta
  = e^{2s^2}\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\left(\sqrt{2}\,s\right),
  \label{eq:zerolagavg}
\end{equation}
which gives $1$ as $s\to 0$ (no suppression) and $\to 0$ as $s\to\infty$
via the standard asymptotic $e^{x^2}\mathrm{erfc}(x)\sim 1/(x\sqrt{\pi})$
(full suppression, $g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(0)\to 1$). So
\begin{equation}
  g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(0)
    = 1 + e^{2s^2}\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\left(\sqrt{2}\,s\right),
    \qquad s\equiv\sigma_0/\tau_c,
  \label{eq:g2zero}
\end{equation}
and the zero-lag bunching peak is suppressed below its ideal value of 2 by
\begin{equation}
  \delta g^{(2)}
  \equiv 2 - g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(0)
  = 1 - e^{2s^2}\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\left(\sqrt{2}\,s\right),
  \label{eq:deltag2}
\end{equation}
giving
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{S}(0) = -\,\delta g^{(2)} \leq 0.
  \label{eq:S0ext}
\end{equation}
This sign is qualitatively distinct from the point-source result
$\mathcal{S}(\tau\neq 0)\geq 0$ of Section~\ref{sec:siegert}: a negative
zero-lag Siegert discrepancy is therefore a sign-flip diagnostic that
distinguishes spatially resolved from unresolved foam signatures.
In the point-source limit $\Phi_{\rm eff}\to 1$, $\sigma_0\to 0$,
$\delta g^{(2)}\to 0$, and $\mathcal{S}(0)\to 0$, recovering the point-source result.
Since $\sigma_0^2\propto(1-\Phi_{\rm eff})$ and $\Phi_{\rm eff}$ decreases
as the source angular diameter increases at fixed $D$ and $r_\perp$, more
extended sources experience greater suppression of the bunching peak and a
more negative Siegert discrepancy at zero lag.

For the astrophysically more representative Voigt-derived $g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)
= e^{-|\tau|/\tau_L}\,e^{-\tau^2/2\tau_G^2}$ introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:g1} (Lorentzian of width $\tau_L$ convolved with a Gaussian
of width $\tau_G$, modelling natural plus Doppler/turbulent broadening), the
intrinsic Siegert relation gives $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\tau)-1 = e^{-2|\tau|/\tau_L}\,
e^{-\tau^2/\tau_G^2}$.
The zero-lag convolution over $\Delta\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_0^2)$ has the same
structure as the first-order Voigt convolution of Eqs.~\eqref{eq:voigt_params}--\eqref{eq:g1obs_voigt},
with $\sigma_\Delta\to\sigma_0$ and the Lorentzian width halved
($\tau_L\to\tau_L/2$ inside the kernel, equivalently the factor of $2$ in
$2\sigma_0^2$ below). The effective width is
\begin{equation}
  \tilde\sigma_2^2 = \frac{\sigma_0^2\,\tau_G^2}{2\sigma_0^2+\tau_G^2},
  \label{eq:sigmatilde}
\end{equation}
yielding
\begin{equation}
  g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(0) - 1
  = \frac{\tilde\sigma_2}{\sigma_0}\,e^{2\tilde s_2^2}\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\left(\sqrt{2}\,\tilde s_2\right),
  \qquad \tilde s_2\equiv\tilde\sigma_2/\tau_L.
  \label{eq:g2zero_voigt}
\end{equation}
In the limit $\tau_G\to\infty$ (pure Lorentzian), $\tilde\sigma_2\to\sigma_0$ and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2zero_voigt} reduces to Eq.~\eqref{eq:g2zero}.
In the limit $\tau_L\to\infty$ (pure Gaussian), $\tilde s_2\to 0$ and
$g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(0)-1 = \tilde\sigma_2/\sigma_0 = \tau_G/\sqrt{2\sigma_0^2+\tau_G^2}$,
with no $O(s)$ correction.

In the narrow-band limit $\sigma_\ell/(c\tau_c)\ll 1$
(Section~\ref{sec:narrowband}), $\sigma_0\ll\tau_c$ for any $\Phi_{\rm eff}$.
Substituting $u=\Delta/\sigma_0$ and expanding $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(\sigma_0 u)-1$
for small $\sigma_0$, the convolution integral yields to first order in
$s=\sigma_0/\tau_c$:
\begin{equation}
  g^{(2)}_{\rm obs}(0) - 1
  \approx \bigl[g^{(2)}_{\rm src}(0)-1\bigr]
          + \sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi}}\,\sigma_0\,g^{(2)\prime}_{\rm src}(0^+),
  \label{eq:g2zero_nb}
\end{equation}
where $g^{(2)\prime}_{\rm src}(0^+)$ denotes the right-derivative of $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}$
at zero lag (the function is non-differentiable at $\tau=0$ for cusped profiles such
as the Lorentzian, so the symmetric derivative does not exist there).
For sources with a smooth (even) profile the right-derivative vanishes and the
leading correction is $O(\sigma_0^2)$; for a Lorentzian,
$g^{(2)\prime}_{\rm src}(0^+) = -2/\tau_c$, recovering
$\delta g^{(2)}\approx 2\sqrt{2/\pi}\,s$, consistent with the small-$s$
expansion of Eq.~\eqref{eq:deltag2}.
For a Voigt profile, the same expansion gives
$\delta g^{(2)} \approx 2\sqrt{2/\pi}\,\sigma_0/\tau_L + (\sigma_0/\tau_G)^2 + \cdots$:
the O(s) term is controlled solely by the Lorentzian component $\tau_L$, and
Gaussian broadening (Doppler or instrumental) enters only at O(s$^2$).
The O(s) scaling therefore survives whenever the line has any Lorentzian component,
provided the line is spectrally resolved at the scale of $\tau_L$.
This is consistent with the sensitivity-scale hierarchy of Section~\ref{sec:scales}:
the zero-lag suppression is small in the narrow-band regime.
Observationally, this implies that a spectrograph transmitting a nearly flat
bandpass (e.g.\ continuum), whose $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}$ is smooth at zero lag,
suppresses the first-order contribution, leaving only an $O(s^2)$ residual.
Maximizing foam sensitivity in $\delta g^{(2)}$ therefore favours emission lines
with a significant Lorentzian component (natural or pressure broadening) that is
spectrally resolved by the instrument; if the line is unresolved, the observed
profile is the instrumental response (approximately Gaussian), the cusp is erased,
and the leading correction reverts to O(s$^2$).

The suppression $\delta g^{(2)}$ arises from a purely spatial mechanism with no
analogue in the point-source case: even at $\tau=0$, photon pairs from different
sky directions $\boldsymbol{\theta}\neq\boldsymbol{\theta}'$ accumulate independent
instantaneous jitters $\delta t(t;\boldsymbol{\theta})\neq\delta t(t;\boldsymbol{\theta}')$
when $b\gg r_\perp$, and their relative offset blurs the $g^{(2)}(0)$ measurement.
Because $|g^{(1)}_{\rm obs}(0)|^2=1$ exactly is unaffected by this spatial jitter,
the two observables are controlled by different mechanisms and $\mathcal{S}(0)<0$.
This does not violate any fundamental inequality: the bound $\mathcal{S}\geq 0$
established in Section~\ref{sec:siegert} applies to a single ray, and the
angular mixture of an extended source need not satisfy it.

The sensitivity parameter for $\delta g^{(2)}$ is
$s = \sigma_0/\tau_c = \sqrt{2(1-\Phi_{\rm eff})}\,\sigma_\phi(\Delta\lambda/\lambda)$;
numerical values across $\alpha$ are tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:comparison}.

\subsection{Inversion Procedure for $r_\perp$}
\label{sec:rperp_inversion}

Within the small-$s$, narrow-band regime, the linear scaling
$\delta g^{(2)} \approx 2\sqrt{2/\pi}\,\sigma_0/\tau_L$ together with the
definition of $\sigma_0$ gives a direct relation between the measured
zero-lag bunching suppression and the effective transverse coherence factor:
\begin{equation}
  1 - \Phi_{\rm eff}
  = \frac{\pi}{16}\,
    \frac{(\delta g^{(2)})^2}{\sigma_\phi^2}\,
    \left(\frac{\lambda}{\Delta\lambda_L}\right)^{\!2},
  \label{eq:PhiEffInversion}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\lambda_L$ is the bandwidth of the Lorentzian component of the
source line. The right-hand side contains only the directly observed
quantities $\delta g^{(2)}$, $\Delta\lambda_L$, and $\lambda$, together with
the foam variance $\sigma_\phi(D,\alpha)$ that is independently constrained
by the point-source Siegert discrepancy $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$ at the same
distance (Section~\ref{sec:siegert}).

Once $\Phi_{\rm eff}$ is fixed by Eq.~\eqref{eq:PhiEffInversion},
Eq.~\eqref{eq:PhiEff} becomes a one-parameter equation for $r_\perp$ given
the source brightness profile $I(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ (which is known
independently from imaging or from the parent stellar model) and an assumed
functional form for $\Phi$. For a circularly symmetric source of angular
diameter $\theta_{\rm src}$ and a kernel $\Phi(x)$ that decays on a scale of
order unity, $\Phi_{\rm eff}$ depends on the dimensionless ratio
$\xi \equiv D\theta_{\rm src}/r_\perp$ alone, monotonically interpolating
between $\Phi_{\rm eff}=1$ at $\xi\ll 1$ (unresolved) and $\Phi_{\rm eff}\to 0$
at $\xi\gg 1$ (fully resolved); inverting numerically yields $r_\perp$.
Limits of validity: Eq.~\eqref{eq:PhiEffInversion} assumes the small-$s$
expansion (Section~\ref{sec:zerolag}), the cross-ray Gaussian approximation
(Section~\ref{sec:approx}), and a spectrally resolved Lorentzian component
$\Delta\lambda_L$; the breakdown at $D\theta_{\rm src} \sim r_\perp$ noted
in Section~\ref{sec:approx} caps the achievable precision in the intermediate
angular-size regime.

Joint measurements of point-like and extended sources at the same redshift
break the residual $(\sigma_\phi, r_\perp)$ degeneracy: the Siegert
discrepancy probes $\sigma_\phi$ alone, while $\delta g^{(2)}$ probes the
combination $\sigma_\phi^2\,(1-\Phi_{\rm eff})$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:PhiEffInversion}.

\section{Discriminants and Self-Calibration}
\label{sec:discriminants}
% ==============================================================

The two principal observables developed in this paper --- the point-source
Siegert discrepancy $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$ of Section~\ref{sec:siegert} and the
extended-source zero-lag bunching suppression $\delta g^{(2)}$ of
Section~\ref{sec:zerolag} --- share two structural features that distinguish
foam from conventional astrophysical phase screens, and a third feature that
removes the need for absolute flux or source-spectrum calibration to detect a
non-zero signal.

\emph{Distance scaling.} The mathematical structure of the foam suppression
factor $\mathcal{F}_{\rm foam}$ is identical to that of any Gaussian random
phase screen, but the $D$-scaling is foam-specific: $\sigma_\phi^2 \propto
D^{2(1-\alpha)}$ ($D^1$ for $\alpha=1/2$, $D^{2/3}$ for $\alpha=2/3$, $D^0$ for
$\alpha=1$), whereas a thin intervening screen scales as $D$ and Kolmogorov
turbulence as $D^{11/6}$; the holographic and conservative foam cases predict
sub-linear or flat growth qualitatively different from any known astrophysical
screen. $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$ inherits the full $D^{2(1-\alpha)}$ scaling
through the variance factor $\sigma_\phi^2$, while $\delta g^{(2)}\propto
\sigma_\phi$ scales as $D^{1-\alpha}$; the resulting $2:1$ exponent ratio at
fixed $\alpha$ provides the falsifiability test of Section~\ref{sec:approx}.

\emph{Achromaticity.} Geometrically, spacetime foam jitter ($\delta
t=\Delta\ell/c$) is independent of $\lambda$, in contrast to plasma-based
effects which scale as $\lambda^2$. As discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:intro}, the existing LIV time-of-flight bounds do not
constrain this achromatic jitter; the principal chromatic alternative the
achromaticity discriminant rules out is therefore plasma propagation. The
discriminant applies equally to both observables. We also note that for foam
models with long-range transverse correlations, image blurring is strongly
suppressed~\cite{LeeZurekChen2024}, so the coherence observables developed
here may be the only channel sensitive to such models~\cite{Carlip2023}.

\emph{Self-calibration.} Both observables are constructed as deviations from
canonical values that hold for any Gaussian chaotic source: $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$
measures the departure of $g^{(2)}-1$ from $|g^{(1)}|^2$ (zero in the absence
of foam, regardless of the source spectrum), and $\delta g^{(2)}$ measures the
departure of $g^{(2)}(0)$ from $2$ (likewise zero in the absence of cross-ray
jitter). \emph{Detection} of a non-zero deviation therefore requires no
separate flux calibration or source model. Quantitative interpretation does
still depend on the source spectrum: the shape of $\mathcal{S}(\tau)$ retains
a $|g^{(1)}_{\rm src}(\tau)|^2$ prefactor, and the magnitude of $\delta
g^{(2)}$ depends on whether the line has a Lorentzian cusp at zero lag
(Section~\ref{sec:zerolag}). Self-calibration mitigates the
visibility--foam degeneracy at the detection stage; it does not eliminate
source-model dependence in the inversion to foam parameters.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We have developed a theoretical framework for the imprint of spacetime foam --- characterized
by the Ng--Perlman exponent $\alpha$ --- on both first- and second-order optical coherence. The 
central finding is that spacetime foam jitter produces two classes of observable 
violation of relations that hold for all Gaussian chaotic sources: a deviation from the 
Siegert relation, $g^{(2)}(\tau) - 1 = |g^{(1)}(\tau)|^2$, and a suppression of the zero-lag 
bunching peak below its canonical value of $g^{(2)}(0) = 2$. These second-order observables 
are complementary to the fringe visibility and Strehl ratio tests based on first-order 
coherence that have already been applied to constrain foam models.
For the holographic model ($\alpha = 2/3$), all such observables lie well within the
currently inaccessible parameter space: the first-order tests probe effects at the
${\sim}10^{-8}$ level, while the second-order observables are characteristically smaller
at ${\sim}10^{-11}$ (a recent analysis of GRB\,221009A claims consistency with holographic
foam blurring~\cite{SteinbringNg2026}, though without formal statistical significance).
The predicted signals approach but do not clearly exceed the systematic floors of
${\sim}10^{-9}$--$10^{-10}$ anticipated for next-generation optical intensity
interferometers, and detection would require both favorable foam parameters and
significant advances in controlling detector systematics
(Fig.~\ref{fig:observability}).

The advantage of the second-order approach lies not in raw sensitivity but in two distinct
features: the Siegert discrepancy is self-calibrating --- any deviation of
$\mathcal{S}(\tau) = g^{(2)}(\tau)-1-|g^{(1)}(\tau)|^2$ from zero is
unambiguously non-Gaussian-chaotic, requiring no separate flux calibration or
source model to detect; and for extended sources whose line profile has a
Lorentzian component (natural or pressure broadening), the zero-lag bunching
suppression $\delta g^{(2)}$ scales linearly with $s = \sigma_0/\tau_L$ --- a
consequence of the cusp in $g^{(2)}_{\rm src}$ at zero lag --- so that despite
being a second-order coherence measurement, $\delta g^{(2)}$ is not doubly
suppressed relative to the first-order observables.

For the fiducial parameters $D = 1$~Gpc, $\lambda = 500$~nm,
$\Delta\lambda/\lambda = 10^{-3}$, $A_\alpha = 1$: the $\alpha = 1/2$ case gives
$\sigma_\phi \approx 280$ (fringes completely erased) and is already excluded by
fringe-visibility measurements~\cite{Perlman2015,Christiansen2011,2003ApJ...587L...1R}.
For the holographic model $\alpha = 2/3$, $\sigma_\phi \sim 10^{-8}$, so
temporal point-source observables are negligibly small with current instrumentation.
However, in the fully resolved limit $\Phi_{\rm eff} \to 0$ (corresponding to
$D\Delta\theta \gg r_\perp$), the extended-source zero-lag bunching suppression
scales linearly rather than quadratically with the small parameter $s = \sigma_0/\tau_c$
and yields $\delta g^{(2)} \approx 5.7\times10^{-11}$, evaluated in the dynamical
limit $C_\alpha\to 0$ (a conditional upper bound). Since $s\propto\Delta\lambda/\lambda$,
broader-band observations would increase $\delta g^{(2)}$ proportionally; for the
holographic model the narrow-band validity condition
$\sigma_\phi\cdot(\Delta\lambda/\lambda)\ll 1$ is trivially satisfied at any
physically realizable bandwidth, so there is no approximation barrier to exploiting
a broader bandpass, though a controlled calculation in the broadband regime ---
where the carrier/envelope decomposition underlying the Siegert violation is no
longer rigorous --- remains an open problem.

In any intensity interferometry measurement, a finite number $M$ of collected
spatiotemporal modes contributes a shot-noise floor $\delta g^{(2)}_{\rm floor}
\sim M^{-1}$, and the foam detectability condition $\sigma_0/\tau_c \gtrsim M^{-1}$
should be quoted alongside any observational upper bound on $\sigma_\ell(D)$
derived from a non-detection.

\subsection{Connection to Existing Radio-Astronomy Pipelines}
\label{sec:radio}

The $g^{(2)}(0)$ observable developed here is already routinely computed in radio astronomy
under different names: spectral kurtosis~\cite{NitaGary2007,NitaGary2010}, used for
real-time RFI excision at the VLA, GBT, LOFAR, and CHIME, is constructed to estimate
$g^{(2)}(0)-1$ from channelized power samples (returning unity in expectation for a
stationary Gaussian field, up to finite-sample bias corrections), as does the modulation
index $m^2 \equiv \mathrm{Var}(I)/\langle I\rangle^2$, which equals $g^{(2)}(0)-1$
for stationary, noise-corrected intensity samples and is used to characterize single-pulse
intensity statistics of pulsars and FRBs~\cite{Weltevrede2006}.
The Siegert-violation framework developed here therefore translates directly to existing
radio data products.
The achievable sensitivity at radio wavelengths is set by the same combination
$s \propto \sigma_\ell(D)\,\Delta\nu/c$ that controls the optical case, but with a
modal shot-noise floor and systematic floor (RFI residuals, digitizer nonlinearity).

\subsection{Cosmological Evolution and Future Directions}
\label{sec:cosmo_outlook}

We have treated $\sigma_\ell^2(D)$ as static, justified by the negligible
fractional change $H_0 T\sim 10^{-10}$ over realistic observation
durations. If $\alpha$ or $A_\alpha$ carries a redshift dependence,
sources at different redshifts will not trace a simple power-law
$\sigma_\phi(D)$, and the distance-scaling discriminants of
Section~\ref{sec:extended} generalize to a non-stationary,
redshift-resolved framework that we leave to future work.
